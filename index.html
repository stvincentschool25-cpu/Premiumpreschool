Frequently Asked Questions

Answers to common questions about preschool and early childhood development.

What is the ideal age to start preschool?

Most children are ready for preschool between ages 2 and 3. At this stage, they've typically developed basic communication skills, can follow simple instructions, and are beginning to show interest in playing with peers. Our playgroup program starts at 1.5 years for children who show readiness for structured social interaction.

Ready to find the perfect program for your child?

How can I prepare my toddler for preschool?

To make the transition smooth, regularly remind your child of the fun and happy things about school — it builds confidence and excitement. You can also prepare your child by establishing routines, reading books about preschool, practicing separation for short periods, and encouraging independence with simple tasks like putting on shoes or using the bathroom. We also offer orientation sessions where you and your child can visit the classroom together before the first day.

Want personalized preparation guidance?

What should I look for in a quality preschool?

Key indicators of quality preschools include: low student-teacher ratios, qualified and caring staff, a safe and stimulating environment, a balanced curriculum that includes play, clear communication with parents, and happy, engaged children. We're proud to meet all these criteria at St. Vincent's.

See our quality standards firsthand!

How does preschool help with separation anxiety?

Our teachers are trained to help children through separation anxiety with comforting routines, engaging activities, and gradual transitions. We recommend starting with shorter days and building up, establishing a consistent goodbye ritual, and reassuring your child that you'll return. Most children adjust within a few weeks as they form bonds with teachers and classmates.

Need help with separation anxiety?

What is the role of play in preschool learning?

Play is the primary way young children learn. Through play, children develop cognitive, physical, social, and emotional skills. Structured and unstructured play activities in our curriculum help children practice problem-solving, build vocabulary, develop fine and gross motor skills, learn to cooperate with peers, and express creativity.
